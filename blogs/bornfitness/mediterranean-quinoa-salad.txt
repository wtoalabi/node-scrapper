Find better options like this quinoa-based dish to fuel your body. \nThe blandness in your diet can usually be blamed on one of three things: a lack of time, creativity, or variety.\nAn easy way to solve all three issues: a good side dish, which can be added to almost any meal or enjoyed as a snack. Enter the mediterranean quinoa salad.\nOften mistaken as a grain, quinoa—pronounced keen-wah—is actually a seed. A complete protein and full of minerals, quinoa’s high-quality nutritional profile is undeniable. The taste, on the other hand, can be pretty bland.\nSure, eating basic nutrient-dense foods can help you with weight loss, but why suffer? This quick and easy recipe, created by Liv Langdon, Born Fitness meal prep specialist and founder of Liv Lang & Prosper, can be made in less than 15 minutes.\nIf you’re a vegetarian, this is a go-to balanced dish with protein-packed quinoa and almonds. For the rest of us, add a grilled chicken breast or fish on top and lunch is ready to go.\nIngredients\n\n2c cooked quinoa*\n¼ c sundried tomatoes, sliced\n½ c artichoke heart quarters, in water\n1/3 c almond slivers, lightly toasted\n2 tsp garlic, minced\n½ tbsp olive oil\n½ tbsp. grassfed butter, unsalted\n2 fresh basil leaves, thinly sliced\n1/2 lemon, freshly squeezed\n¼ tsp Himalayan sea salt\nBlack pepper, to taste\n1 oz. crumbled feta cheese, just for taste (optional)\n\n*Quinoa can be cooked in advanced using water (to keep it vegetarian friendly) or use a low-sodium chicken broth for added flavor.\n\nDirections\n\nIn a large sauté pan on medium heat, add the olive oil and garlic.\nWhen the garlic is slightly toasted, add in the rest of the ingredients except the feta cheese. Stir until well combined.\nCook for five to 10 minutes to let the flavors develop. Stir occasionally.\nTop with crumbled feta cheese, if desired.\nServe up ¼ of the recipe for a light, vegetarian-friendly meal or add a grilled chicken breast to bump up the protein.\nMakes four servings.\n\n\nNutritional Information & Macros\nDietary Information: Gluten free, Vegetarian\n**If you want dairy-free, omit the feta cheese.\nMacros per serving (¼ of the recipe)\n\n222 calories\n10g fat\n25g carbs\n7.5g protein\n\nTotal breakdown for 4 servings\nQuinoa: 444 calories, 8g fat, 80g carbs, 16g protein\nAlmond slivers: 170 calories, 14g fat, 6g carbs, 6g protein\nOlive oil: 60 calories, 7g fat, 0g carbs, 0g protein\nButter: 50 calories, 5.5g fat, 0g carbs, 0g protein\nSundried tomatoes: 34 calories, 0.68 g fat, 7.4g carbs, 2g protein\nArtichoke hearts: 35 calories, 0g fat, 6g carbs, 2g protein\nFeta cheese: 74 calories, 6g fat, 1.2g carbs, 4g protein\nTotal: 889 calories, 41g fat, 100g carbs, 30g protein\nAdam BornsteinAdam Bornstein is a New York Times bestselling author and, according to The Huffington Post, “one of the most inspiring sources in all of health and fitness.” An award-winning writer and editor, Bornstein was the Fitness and Nutrition editor for Men’s Health, Editorial Director at LIVESTRONG.com, and a columnist for SHAPE, Men’s Fitness, and Muscle & Fitness.
